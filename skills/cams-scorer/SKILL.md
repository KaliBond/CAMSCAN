# CAMS Scoring Protocol v2.1

## Civilizational Analysis and Measurement System

### Scoring Layer Only

---

## Purpose

This protocol is for the **scoring stage only** of CAMS analysis.

Its purpose is to guide an evaluator — human or LLM — in assigning evidence-based scores to the **eight CAMS nodes** on four dimensions:

- **Coherence**
- **Capacity**
- **Stress**
- **Abstraction**

This protocol does **not** include downstream calculation.
Do **not** calculate:

- Node Value
- Bond Strength
- system-level averages
- any derived metric

Your task ends when you have produced a complete **8-node scoring snippet** in the required format.

---

## Philosophy & Approach

### Core Principle

You are analysing civilizational cognition through **institutional coordination capacity**.

Think thermodynamically: societies are metabolic systems that acquire, process, store, coordinate, and act on information. Your task is to assess how well each core function is operating under real historical conditions.

### Validator Stance: Evidence-Based Realism

When scoring, adopt the following position:

- **Capacity**: score based on **demonstrated performance**, not theoretical potential
- **Abstraction**: score based on **operational sophistication**, not aspirational complexity
- **Coherence**: score based on **actual coordination**, not formal structure
- **Stress**: score based on **entropy production** — that is, observed breakdown rate — not subjective discomfort

### Key Distinctions

- A node can be **structurally sophisticated** but **functionally impaired**
- A node can be under **heavy pressure** without being highly stressed, if it continues to function coherently
- A node can face **limited pressure** but still show high stress if it is fragmenting or failing

### Scoring Rule

**Score what is operationally true, not what ought to be true, and not what might be possible under better conditions.**

### Independence Rule

Score each node on the evidence for that node.

Do **not** adjust scores to make the total system appear balanced, elegant, symmetrical, or mathematically plausible.
Do **not** anticipate downstream formulas.

---

## Historical Transitions: Special Guidance

Periods of regime change, revolution, war, reform, partition, or institutional transition require special care.

Do not automatically score transitions as either success or collapse. Examine the coordination dynamics.

### During major transitions, ask:

1. **Coherence**: did the node maintain internal coordination through the change, or did it fragment?
2. **Capacity**: could the node still perform its function during the transition?
3. **Stress**: was the transition managed under pressure, or did disorder begin accumulating rapidly?
4. **Abstraction**: did the node retain institutional learning, strategic memory, or adaptive sophistication?

### Calibration examples

**Successful negotiated transition**
Example: South Africa, 1990–94

- Coherence: 6–7
- Capacity: 6–7
- Stress: 6–7

This reflects high pressure, but managed coordination.

**Violent revolutionary collapse**
Example: Russia, 1917–18

- Coherence: 2–3
- Capacity: 2–4
- Stress: 9–10

This reflects fragmentation, functional breakdown, and extreme entropy production.

## Step 1: Define the Eight Nodes

Societies are analysed as performing **eight enduring coordination functions**. These are not merely formal offices or social classes. They are recurrent functional domains that complex societies must perform in some form.

### CAMS Nodes

| Node | Function | What to Assess |
|---|---|---|
| **Helm** | Strategic coordination and executive decision-making | Can leadership formulate direction, align institutions, and execute decisions? |
| **Shield** | Security provision and force projection | Can the system maintain order, defend itself, and project coercive force when required? |
| **Lore** | Knowledge synthesis and cultural legitimation | Can knowledge institutions generate, preserve, interpret, and legitimate shared understanding? |
| **Stewards** | Resource ownership and capital allocation | Can wealth-holding or asset-controlling actors allocate resources productively to system needs? |
| **Craft** | Skilled production and professional coordination | Can specialised workers and professionals deliver sophisticated functions reliably? |
| **Hands** | Labour execution and basic production | Can the labouring base mobilise and perform large-scale practical work? |
| **Archive** | Institutional memory and information storage | Can critical information be preserved, retrieved, and transmitted across time? |
| **Flow** | Commerce, trade, and circulation | Can goods, services, money, and logistical throughput move effectively through the system? |

## Identifying Nodes in Historical Context

For each society and year:

1. Identify the specific institutions, groups, or structures performing each function
2. Recognise that one institution may perform multiple functions
3. Focus on **functional performance**, not formal titles

## Step 2: Assign Scores on the Four Dimensions

All scores are integers from **1 to 10**.

## COHERENCE

### Internal Unity and Coordination

**Question:**
How well do the parts of this node coordinate with one another?

**Thermodynamic interpretation:**
Low coherence means high internal disorder.

| Score | Description | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **1–2** | Fragmented | open internal conflict, paralysis, no shared purpose |
| **3–4** | Divided | major factions, inconsistent action, competing agendas |
| **5–6** | Functional | coordination works despite tensions, generally aligned |
| **7–8** | Integrated | strong internal coordination, shared purpose, aligned execution |
| **9–10** | Optimal | near-seamless alignment and coordination |

### Scoring guidance

- Do not confuse **autocracy** with coherence
- Do not confuse **consensus language** with operational coordination
- Look for actual ability to align action across the node

## CAPACITY

### Resources and Effectiveness

**Question:**
How effectively can this node perform its function?

**Thermodynamic interpretation:**
Capacity is the usable free energy available for work.

| Score | Description | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **1–2** | Collapsed | cannot function, institutional failure, no effective resources |
| **3–4** | Insufficient | struggling to perform core tasks, serious shortfalls |
| **5–6** | Adequate | can perform core functions, but with limited reserve |
| **7–8** | Strong | effective, well-resourced, reliable performance |
| **9–10** | Dominant | exceptional capability, overwhelming effectiveness |

### Scoring guidance

- Score **performance**, not nominal resources
- Score **effectiveness**, not size
- A large but dysfunctional node may score low
- A small but highly effective node may score high

## STRESS

### Entropic Load and Breakdown Rate

**Question:**
How much disorder is this node producing? How fast is it breaking down?

**Thermodynamic definition:**
Stress is the rate of disorder production, not the mere presence of external challenge.

| Score | Description | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **1–2** | Thriving | resilient, stable, operating below limit, strengthening |
| **3–4** | Stable | routine challenges handled, no serious breakdown |
| **5–6** | Pressured | strain visible, but function maintained |
| **7–8** | Strained | breakdown emerging, coherence degrading |
| **9–10** | Rupture | catastrophic breakdown, failure accelerating |

### Scoring guidance

- Do not score how hard conditions are
- Score how much **breakdown** is occurring
- Look for coherence loss, capacity degradation, institutional disintegration, accelerating failure

## ABSTRACTION

### Learning, Sophistication, and Information Processing

**Question:**
How sophisticated are this node’s operations and learning capacity?

**Thermodynamic interpretation:**
Abstraction is the ability to compress, process, model, and adapt using information.

| Score | Description | Observable Indicators |
|---|---|---|
| **1–2** | Primitive | reactive only, no institutional learning, no usable memory |
| **3–4** | Basic | simple procedures, limited adaptation, narrow repertoire |
| **5–6** | Functional | some learning, established systems, incremental adaptation |
| **7–8** | Sophisticated | strong learning systems, adaptive strategy, innovation capacity |
| **9–10** | Frontier | cutting-edge knowledge, system-transforming innovation capacity |

### Scoring guidance

- This is not a proxy for formal education alone
- A credential-rich node that cannot learn may score low
- A simple but adaptive node may score moderately high
- Look for institutional learning, strategic modelling, adaptive sophistication, innovation, and memory

## Step 3: Research and Evidence Gathering

### Primary sources

Where available, prioritise:

- administrative records
- legislation
- military records
- fiscal and production data
- trade and price data
- newspapers and journals
- letters, diaries, memoirs
- institutional archives
- religious and cultural texts

### Secondary sources

Use:

- academic histories
- political and economic analysis
- institutional studies
- biographies
- local and regional histories
- scholarly syntheses

---

## Step 4: Evidence Questions for Each Node

For each node, gather evidence under five headings.

### 1. Structural identification
- Who performs this function?
- What institutions or actors carry it?
- What resources do they control?

### 2. Coherence
- Are there major internal factions?
- Are the parts aligned or competing?
- Is action coordinated or contradictory?

### 3. Capacity
- Can this node actually perform its role?
- What usable resources does it command?
- What does it reliably achieve?

### 4. Stress
- Is the node holding together?
- Is capacity degrading?
- Are failures accumulating?
- Is disorder accelerating?

### 5. Abstraction
- How sophisticated are its methods?
- Can it learn, adapt, and remember?
- Does it innovate or merely react?

---

## Step 5: Scoring Procedure

For each node and each metric:

1. Gather evidence from multiple sources
2. Compare evidence against the rubric
3. Assign the best-fit integer score from 1 to 10
4. Check for internal consistency across the four dimensions
5. Move to the next node

### Important

Cross-checking is allowed. Score-smoothing is not.

You may ask whether a score is plausible in relation to the evidence on the other dimensions, but do not artificially force neat patterns.
